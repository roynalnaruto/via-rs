The abstract page lays out the security↔correctness tension in general. This page resolves it concretely: it derives a parameter set that provably clears ≥120-bit classical security on every exposed lattice instance while keeping the Appendix-C noise budget under \(2^{-40}\), explains why each choice is forced rather than picked, and ships it as the SECURE_PARAMS preset. The companion benchmarks page measures what it costs. Throughout, “provable” means the standard lattice-crypto sense: the lattice estimator reports every known attack at \(\ge 2^{120}\).

↑

The gap — what the published parameters actually deliver

VIA exposes two independent lattice instances to the server, and the scheme’s security is the minimum of the two1: the large ring \(\mathcal R_{n_1,q_1}\) (the query, conversion keys, and gate keys), and the small ring \(\mathcal R_{n_2,q_3}\) carried by the ring-switching key under the small-ring secret \(S_2\). Running each through the lattice estimator inference2:

Instance \((n,\ \log_2 q)\) Secret Estimate (classical)
Large ring\((2048,\ 75)\)Gaussian \(\sigma{=}32\) paper109.8 bits
Small ring\((512,\ 23)\)Gaussian \(\sigma{=}26\) paper110.2 bits
Large ring\((2048,\ 75)\)ternary code101.4 bits
Small ring\((512,\ 23)\)ternary code87.0 bits ← binding

Two facts fall out, and they are kinder to the paper than the headline “110 vs 72” suggests inference:

  • The paper’s 110-bit claim reproduces — both rings land at \(\approx\!110\) under the paper’s Gaussian distributions. The estimator agrees with Liu et al. on their own parameters.
  • But via-rs runs ternary keys, not the paper’s Gaussians (the Gaussian preset is inert — A4). That substitution drops the large ring to 101 and the small ring to 87. The small ring at \(n_2=512\) is the binding instance, and no choice of modulus rescues 512 dimensions to 120 bits. (Yue et al.’s measured ~72/88 were taken on the C++ owniai/VIA, a third parameter set again3.)

Either way, the shipped code is an ~87-bit scheme, ~33 bits short of a 120-bit target.

Method — two estimators, cross-checked

A credible “≥120-bit” claim has to clear two independent gates, computed with tools, not asserted:

  • Security. The lattice estimator (Albrecht et al.), classical core-SVP cost model, run on each exposed \((n,q,\chi_s,\chi_e)\). The binding attack here is primal bdd/usvp across the board. Calibration check: \((n{=}2048,\ q{=}2^{54},\ \text{ternary})\) estimates at 126.3 bits, matching the Homomorphic Encryption Security Standard’s 128-bit edge for that dimension2.
  • Correctness. The paper’s Appendix-C noise recursion, reimplemented and validated against the authors’ own erratum: with the erratum gadget bases it reproduces \(\log_2 P_{\text{fail}}\approx -39.8\) against the paper’s \((q_3{-}q_4)/p\) threshold — matching the authors’ stated \(-43.4\)4. Decoding is then evaluated at the final modulus \(q_4\) (threshold \(\Delta/2 = q_4/2p\)), which Yue Chen argues — and we concur — is the correct decode point (A5).

Why both rings must grow (and ternary is forced)

The instantiation below raises both ring dimensions. Each increase is forced by a collision of constraints, not chosen for comfort — which is exactly why the 120-bit profile departs from the paper’s.

1. The large ring cannot stay at \(n_1=2048\)

At \(n_1=2048\) with ternary keys, 120-bit security needs \(\log_2 q_1 \le 56\) inference (the estimator gives 126 bits at \(q_1{=}2^{54}\), ~118 at \(2^{56}\), and only 101 at the published \(2^{75}\)). But \(q_1\) is also bounded from below by correctness: the DMux output and the first-dimension/CMux sum must survive modulo \(q_1\)/\(q_2\) before they are switched down. Tracing the Appendix-C variance, the first-dimension–CMux noise at \(q_2\) only stays under the message budget \(q_2/2p\) when \(q_1 \gtrsim 2^{60}\) inference5 (the smaller \(q_1\) is, the less the DMux noise is scaled down at the \(q_1\!\to\!q_2\) switch, so it overflows \(q_2\)).

Security wants \(q_1 \le 2^{56}\); correctness wants \(q_1 \ge 2^{60}\). No \(q_1\) satisfies both at \(n_1=2048\).

Because ring dimensions must be powers of two for the NTT, the next feasible dimension is \(n_1=4096\) — where 120-bit security tolerates \(\log_2 q_1\) up to \(\sim\!112\), so the published \(2^{75}\) sits comfortably inside the window that the correctness lower bound (\(\gtrsim 2^{63}\) at \(n_1{=}4096\)) opens.

2. The small ring cannot stay at \(n_2=512\)

\(n_2=512\) is only 87-bit at \(q_3=2^{23}\). One could try to buy security by shrinking \(q_3\) (smaller modulus ⇒ harder lattice at fixed \(n\)): the estimator confirms \((512,\ 2^{14})\) reaches ~159 bits. But \(q_4 < q_3\) always, so \(q_3 = 2^{14}\) caps the response modulus at \(q_4 \le 2^{13}\) — and (see below) the \(n_1{=}4096\) noise needs \(q_4 = 2^{15}\) to decode. Shrinking \(q_3\) to secure \(n_2{=}512\) breaks the decode; keeping \(q_3\) large enough to decode leaves \(n_2{=}512\) at 87 bits. The smallest power-of-two that resolves the squeeze is \(n_2=1024\): it is 174-bit even at \(q_3=2^{23}\), leaving full room for \(q_4=2^{15}\).

3. The secret must be ternary — which is what makes (1) bite

The final \(q_3\!\to\!q_4\) mod-switch adds a rounding term of variance \((1+n_1\theta_S)/12\), where \(\theta_S=\mathbb E[s_i^2]\). With the paper’s Gaussian secret (\(\sigma_S{=}32,\ \theta_S{=}1024\)) this term alone is \(\sigma\approx418\) at \(n_1{=}4096\) — far above the decode budget \(\Delta/2 = q_4/2p = 128\). With a ternary secret (\(\theta_S=\tfrac23\)) it is \(\sigma\approx15\), comfortably under inference. So the scheme only decodes correctly at this \(q_4\) because the key is ternary — the very substitution that weakened security in the first place. 120-bit must therefore be reached with ternary keys (hence by raising \(n\)), not by adopting the paper’s wide Gaussian.

This closes the loop between three findings that looked separate: the inert Gaussian preset (A4), the decode-threshold question (A5), and Yue’s “increase \(q_4\)” remark are one mechanism.

inference The estimate overshoots 120 substantially (174–214 bits). That margin is not waste — it is forced by the power-of-two dimension: \(n_1=2048\) tops out at 101 bits and the next step, 4096, lands at 214. There is no dimension in between, so a clean ≥120 inevitably overshoots. The binding instance is now the small ring at 174 bits.

Correctness check — the budget still closes

inference Evaluating the Appendix-C recursion at the secure parameters, with ternary keys and erratum gadget bases, decoding at \(q_4\):

Check Value Target Pass?
\(q_4\)-decode failure (\(\Delta/2 = 512\))\(\log_2 P_{\text{fail}} = -43.8\)\(\le -40\)✓
\(q_2\)-survival (first-dim/CMux, mod \(q_2\))\(\approx 2^{-42}\) (margin \(x=6.1\))\(\le 2^{-40}\)✓
end-to-end (dominated by \(q_2\)-survival)\(\approx 2^{-41.5}\)\(\le 2^{-40}\)✓

code The pipeline is also confirmed empirically end-to-end at these dimensions: a full query→answer→recover round-trip at \(n_1{=}4096,\ n_2{=}1024\) with the new moduli, \(q_4{=}2^{15}\), ternary keys and erratum gadgets — exercising the n4096 LWE→RLWE cascade — recovers the correct record (client_server_e2e_secure, ~17 s release). On a small \(2\times2\) database the accumulated noise is far below the 32 GiB budget above, so this witnesses the mechanics (and the integration), not the worst-case budget.

Two honest caveats inference: (a) the margin is tight — the binding \(q_2\)-survival term sits at \(2^{-42}\), only ~2 bits past target. Raising the CMux gadget length \(\ell\!:\!2\to3\) restores a wide margin but enlarges the gate keys and adds a polynomial multiply per CMux level (a correctness-for-performance trade the benchmarks would reflect). (b) \(q_4=2^{15}\) was chosen as the smallest power-of-two clearing the decode: \(2^{14}\) gives only \(-41.3\) and the failure probability saturates near \(-44\) as \(q_4\) grows (the \(q_3\!\to\!q_4\) noise grows quadratically in \(q_4\) while the threshold grows linearly), so there is no benefit past \(2^{15}\).

What it costs

Raising both rings is not free; quantifying the hit is the whole point of the exercise. In brief inference (measured in full on the benchmarks page):

  • Compute ~2.2×. The multiplicative core (eval-backed gadget_product) is \(O(n\log n)\): measured 6159 → 13341 µs/op going \(n{=}2048\to4096\), a 2.17× factor.
  • Conversion key ~2.2×. The LWE→RLWE cascade key grows 24.75 MB → 54.00 MB (one extra fold).
  • Response ~2.5×. The answer is an \(n_2\)-coefficient ciphertext at \(q_4\): \(1024\cdot15\) vs \(512\cdot12\) bits.

So real 120-bit security changes VIA-C’s advertised profile by roughly 2× across compute, key size, and response size — the paper’s headline figures are a ~87–101-bit operating point, not a 120-bit one.

Using it

code The preset is wired end-to-end behind the same const-generic machinery as REALISTIC_PARAMS: 

use via_protocol::{SECURE_PARAMS, ViaCSecure120Params};
// n1=4096, n2=1024, q1=2^74.7 (RNS), q2=2^34, q3=2^23, q4=2^15, p=16,
// erratum gadgets (18073/307/4), ternary keys, security_param = 120.
assert_eq!(SECURE_PARAMS.security_param, 120);

Unlike the published presets, the \(120\) here is witnessed: the estimator script and the noise-budget calculator that produced every number above are the audit trail. An implementer should still re-run both on the exact \((n,q,\sigma)\) they ship — the one number you must never hard-code is the one that says you are safe.
